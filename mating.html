Mating Behavior

How do they choose mates?


In sexually reproducing species, choosing a mate is one of the most crucial decisions determining an individualâ€™s fitness. These decisions also determine when differentiation and gene flow between populations are possible. We study how insect species developing in and adapting to novel environments influences mating behavior.


We do this by conducting mate trials. We use lab-reared bugs and conduct mate trials by housing groups of adults (for example, two males and one female) in a large bug home. Soapberry bug males can be quite aggressive in attempting to mate with both females and other males, but an uninterested female can typically throw off courting males. Once in copula, mating pairs can remain connected by the genitalia for hours or even days. During mate trials, we observe each of these mating behaviors. By manipulating who is included in each mate trial, we can understand how the developmental environment, morphology, and evolutionary history of a bug influences its reproductive success.
